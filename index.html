Getting a pet for better mental health

As stated by World Health Organisation, there are many threats to mental health, such as economic and social inequalities, and more recently, the Covid-19 Pandemic. Poor mental health can kill. This is seen by suicide being the fourth leading cause of death among 15- to 19-year-olds. People with severe mental health conditions can die prematurely ‚Äì as much as two decades early - due to preventable physical conditions. Yet, there is still a considerable gap between people needing care and those with access to care. As such, we hope for the benefits below to promote owning a pet as a way to improve mental health, especially for those who are afraid of going for therapy due to its stigma.

Below are some graphs to further convince you of the benefits of owning a pet.

source: Friends with benefits: On the positive consequences of pet ownership

This graph illustrates that pet owners have greater self-esteem, greater levels of exercise and physical fitness, and tend to be less lonely than non-owners. Pet owners generally fared better at well-being outcomes, which are desirable for those with poor mental health.

source: Health Benefits and Health Cost Savings Due to Pets: Preliminary Estimates from an Australian National Survey

Even though the participants from the research paper are Australians, several other studies have confirmed the general thrust of these findings and have further explained the mechanisms through which pets produced better health. For example, Vormbrock and Grossberg (1988) showed that patting a dog directly lowered the blood pressure of people with hypertension.

Getting a pet for elderly

Older people are especially vulnerable to loneliness and social isolation - and it can have a serious effect on health. People can become socially isolated for a variety of reasons, such as getting older or weaker, no longer being the hub of their family, leaving the workplace, the deaths of spouses and friends, or through disability or illness. In Singapore alone, data collected from more than 2,000 older Singapore citizens and permanent residents showed that a third (34 per cent) perceived themselves to be lonely. This proportion increased with age, from 32 per cent among those aged 60‚Äì69 years, to 40 per cent among those aged 80 and above.
